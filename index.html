A Day in Asset Operations — What Actually Happens Behind the Scenes

By Aravind · Asset Operations Intern · Chennai

Most people associate fintech with seamless applications, instant transactions, and clean user interfaces that simplify financial interactions. From the outside, everything appears efficient, fast, and almost effortless. However, what users experience is only a small part of a much larger system. Behind every transaction lies a structured operational layer that ensures money moves accurately, risks are managed effectively, compliance requirements are met, and every stakeholder involved in the process remains aligned. This layer is rarely visible, but it is fundamental to how the system functions.

Working in asset operations has given me direct exposure to this underlying layer, and it has reshaped my understanding of how fintech actually operates. The complexity is not in what is visible to the user, but in how multiple processes, teams, and systems are coordinated in the background. Each step requires attention to detail, clarity of thought, and a structured approach to ensure that execution happens without errors or ambiguity.

A typical day does not begin with execution. It begins with understanding. Every transaction is part of a broader deal structure, and before taking any action, it is important to fully grasp how that structure works. This includes identifying the stakeholders involved, understanding how funds are expected to flow across accounts, evaluating timelines, and recognizing the obligations associated with each party. Without this clarity, even a technically correct transaction can lead to confusion or misalignment later in the process. In asset operations, execution without context introduces risk, which is why alignment comes first.

Financial modeling plays a key role in shaping this understanding. It is not limited to calculating returns, but extends to defining how a deal behaves over time. By structuring inflows and outflows, validating assumptions, and analyzing timelines, financial models act as a blueprint for the transaction. They help ensure that expectations are realistic and aligned across stakeholders. More importantly, they provide a structured way to anticipate potential issues before execution begins. Even minor inconsistencies in a model can create larger operational challenges later, making validation just as critical as creation.

Once the structure is clear, the next step involves formalizing it through documentation. This includes initiating and coordinating agreements such as discounting agreements, indemnity bonds, guarantee agreements, and term sheets. Each of these documents plays a specific role in defining responsibilities, covering risks, and establishing expectations between parties. From an operational perspective, documentation transforms a conceptual deal into something enforceable and accountable. Without it, execution may still occur, but the absence of clearly defined terms increases uncertainty and risk.

Before any movement of funds takes place, the necessary infrastructure must be established. This is where the creation of Virtual Bank Accounts (VBAs) becomes important. VBAs allow transactions to be tracked in a structured manner, ensuring that each flow of funds can be mapped back to a specific deal. Alongside this, KYC processes ensure that all entities involved are verified and compliant with regulatory requirements. These steps may appear procedural, but they form the foundation for transparency and control. Without them, managing and reconciling transactions becomes significantly more complex.

At the same time, backend systems need to be prepared to support the transaction lifecycle. This includes creating vendor IDs and setting up asset cards. These are not merely administrative tasks, but essential components that enable proper tracking, reporting, and data consistency. A well-structured backend ensures that every transaction is recorded accurately and can be traced throughout its lifecycle. In contrast, inadequate setup often leads to repeated issues, inefficiencies, and discrepancies that become difficult to resolve later.

Once all structures, documentation, and systems are in place, execution begins. This phase requires close coordination with finance teams to ensure that funds are allocated correctly, routed to the appropriate accounts, and tracked effectively. Precision is critical at this stage, as even small errors in fund allocation can lead to complications during reconciliation or reporting. Unlike front-end systems where errors are immediately visible, operational discrepancies may only surface later, making it essential to approach each step with care and accuracy.

In parallel with execution, compliance requirements must also be addressed. TDS coordination is a key aspect of this process, ensuring that the appropriate deductions are made, regulatory guidelines are followed, and proper records are maintained. While this layer may not always be visible in day-to-day operations, it plays a crucial role in maintaining the integrity of the system. Ignoring compliance rarely leads to immediate issues, but it almost always results in more significant challenges over time.

Another critical aspect of asset operations is maintaining visibility for stakeholders. This involves providing clear and timely updates on account balances, transaction statuses, and statements. Communication in this context is not just about sharing information, but about building confidence in the system. When stakeholders have clarity, it reduces uncertainty and ensures smoother coordination across all parties involved.

In practice, processes rarely unfold exactly as planned. Delays in approvals, mismatches in data, and incomplete documentation are common challenges. A significant part of the role involves navigating these situations without disrupting the overall flow of operations. This requires adaptability, problem-solving skills, and the ability to make informed decisions even when all information may not be immediately available.

Through this experience, I have come to understand that asset operations sits at the intersection of finance, compliance, and execution. It is a function that demands attention to detail, structured thinking, and effective communication. More importantly, it requires the ability to manage complexity in a way that ensures everything continues to function smoothly in the background.

For most users, fintech feels instant and effortless. Transactions happen quickly, and systems appear to work seamlessly. However, behind that simplicity lies a carefully coordinated framework of processes, controls, and decisions. Asset operations is a key part of this framework, ensuring that every transaction is executed accurately, every requirement is met, and every stakeholder remains aligned.
